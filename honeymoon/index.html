Last Day of the Honeymoon Extravaganza
[ Posted Thu, 16 Feb 2012 10:20:55 ]

Today was the last day of our Honeymoon Extravaganza and the city with the honor of hosting this momentous event is Hong Kong.

We started out today looking for good dim sum.  Matt is still haunted by the stellar dim sum we had at Fook Lam Moon, but I somehow convinced him to try another place in Hong Kong.  We started first at the famous Yung Kee, but they don’t start serving dim sum until 2 pm.  Then we ambled over to the Lin Heung Tea House but I was too intimidated by the process (1. Defeated we headed out to Sham Shui Po, where we were planning to shop anyway.  Sham Shui Po is way off the tourist track, a working-class neighborhood where locals do their shopping.  There, we ended up eating at Tim Ho Wan, which is, it turns out, one of the best places to have dim sum in Hong Kong and the cheapest restaurant in the world with a Michelin star.  We had 8 plates of dim sum and tea for about $15 USD.  

After some light shopping in Sham Shui Po, I went to take a nap and Matt went for a walk to the Hollywood Road and Cat Street antiques markets, followed by the HK Botanical and Zoological Garden.  He also took his 50th ride on the Mid-Levels Escalator.  I told him that he should jut marry the Mid-Levels Escalator and he said maybe he would.

We finished off the day with a lovely dinner in Causeway Bay and an extended tram ride through Hong Kong.

We have had a wonderful time, but we are looking forward to coming home.

Little India, Mustafa Center, Bugis, and the Night Safari
[ Posted Mon, 13 Feb 2012 10:27:43 ]

We started off the day in our usual way, by walking across the street from our hotel to the Maxwell Street Hawker Center and picking up fruit drinks for breakfast.  You choose what ever you’d like from the huge array of beautiful fresh fruit (watermelon, papaya, mango, dragonfruit, guava, jack fruit, to name a few) and they toss it in a blender.  Our favorite so far is watermelon and mint.  Then we caught the subway out to Little India.

Little India was neat, and very colorful.  Among other things, we checked out the Sri Veeramakaliamman Temple — Singapore’s oldest Hindu temple — and the Mustafa Center, which is an incredibly large department store/supermarket for Singapore’s Indian populace.

It’s a short walk from Little India to Bugis and Haji Lane, center of Singapore’s Arab population.  After exploring this area for a bit, we caught the MRT (subway) at Bugis Station (Bugis!) back to our hotel to nap through the heat of the day.  We grabbed dinner at the hawker center across the street, and then headed out for the Night Safari!

The Night Safari is run by the Singapore Zoo and it is a lot of fun.  It starts with a tram ride through the Southeast Asian jungle — headlights off, completely dark — and periodically you come to clearings in the jungle where you may see an animal like a rhino, lion, or elephant, or one of the funky Malaysian or Indonesian deer.  After the tram ride, you are free to explore, and there are pathways that criss-cross the safari area, giving access to many more animals, like leopards, tapirs, and, our favorite, the slow loris.

One of the coolest things was just like an aviary except instead of birds it was fruit bats.  It’s very dark, you are on an elevated walkway through a jungle, there are a few soft lights aimed at the pathway to keep you from falling off, and through the air all around you are BIG bats.  They come very close and you can feel the air from their wings.  Willa was not happy and we did not stay long.

Japan Special Bonus Bathroom Edition
[ Posted Sat, 11 Feb 2012 03:30:25 ]

For those who are curious, we have been keeping track of bathroom facilities in Japan.  If you are sensitive or squeamish, please skip this post entirely as we’ll be using words like “flush”, “spray” and “pooper”.  

Japan might be the most advanced civilization in the world when it comes to bathrooms based on the following guidelines:

1. Bathrooms should be everywhere - on street corners, in parks , in transit stations, on every floor of a department store and every restaurant.

2. Bathrooms should be clean - there’s no tell-tale stink of human excretions in any of these facilities.  Yes, some of them you even have to change shoes to go into.

Shoes at a bathroom in one of the temples.

3. No one wants to hear you do your business - as such, there are “Flush Noise” buttons that play the sound of a flushing toilet for the duration of your stay.  And your business.

Flush noise speakers.

4. No one likes a cold toilet seat - heated toilet seats.  Until you have experienced it, there is no way to describe the luxury of a perpetually heated toilet seat.

This toilet seat is heated - you’ll have to take our word for it.

5. Wiping is no way to get clean - instead, the bathrooms have both “Spray” and “Bidet” functions that shoot pleasantly warm water either straight into your pooper (“Spray”) or along the entire region in question (“Bidet”). 

The control panel, including options to increase/decrease water pressure and temperature on your pooper.

6. No one wants to smell you do your business - deodorizers come in the form of poring water to cover your poop or pee, or a light mist of perfume.

All in all - the only thing that we would look into getting is the heated toilet seat because it’s spoiled all other toilet seats out there.

[ Posted Tue, 07 Feb 2012 07:31:00 ]

Today, we decided to stay close to our hotel, which happens to be in Northern Higashimaya - home to the Tetsugaku no Michi (“Path of Philosophy”) and numerous beautiful gardens, shrines and temples.  Our impression of Kyoto is that you can stop anywhere you want, whether it is a temple, a garden, a shrine or a passageway, and be awed by the scenery around you.  It’s a wonder that they got anything done here back when it was Japan’s capitol, as you could just stop and stare and ponder forever.

Along the Path of Philosophy, we ran into an old Aqueduct, many Zen gardens with raked gravel designs, huge temple gates and beautiful temples set against the misty Higashimaya mountains.  Along the way, we wandered into one of the most beautiful places we’ve ever been - the Oku-no-in.

We then crossed back into urban Kyoto (it’s a nice little city, but that’s not what you come to Kyoto for) and walked down the Ponto-Cho, an alleyway that is crowded on both sides with ancient little restaurants.  The alley is atmospheric with old two-story Japanese buildings and lanterns hanging out front.

From downton Tokyo, we visited Akusaka an ancient area of street markets centered around a big, popular shrine.   It was a very hopping area and hopefully an appetizer for the shrines we will see tomorrow in Nikko.

All around Tokyo
[ Posted Fri, 03 Feb 2012 08:23:00 ]

We started the day back in Shinjuku.  It’s one of the few places in the city where you can pick up your JapanRail pass (which is a good deal and only available to visitors, sort of like the Eurail pass).  After picking up our rail passes, we had udon for lunch.  It was even better than yesterday’s ramen. Again, the drill was: no talking, slurp hard and as fast as possible.

We then caught a subway across town to the Fukugawa Edo Museum — thanks to Tian for the recommendation!  It is a big warehouse inside of which they have reconstructed an Edo-era village, complete with houses, grocers, and sawmill.

You can walk right into many of the houses and open the cupboards and poke around.  You can even awkwardly fold your body (still full of udon) into a sitting position at one of the model dinner tables.

Up the street from the Edo museum we saw a sign for a garden.  So we went in and found an incredible serene place.  Put together during the Edo period and then purchased by Iwasaki Yataro, the founder of Mitsubishi, the garden is now open to the public.  

It’s a beautiful pathway around a small lake full of ducks and carp.  The pathway is lined with boulders that were selected and shipped from all over Japan by Yataro’s wife.  She had good taste — the boulders are really neat looking.

We left the gardens and headed across town to what may be as different a place as you can get in Japan — Akihabara Electric Town.  The center of the world for electric gadgets, knick-knacks and doodads.

For dinner, our hotel recommended to us a sushi bar in the Suidobashi neighborhood.  It was the best sushi Matt has ever had.  Willa doesn’t really appreciate sushi, but she thought it was pretty good.  The tuna was soft and marbled and buttery.  The uni was delicate and spectacular.  We were the only people at the bar, except for two salarymen eating quietly in the corner, so we had a great time trying to communicate with the chef.  He was very excited about his sushi and, though we could not speak each other’s languages, it was fun learning from him the various things we were eating.  He enjoyed showing us how fresh his food was and, at one point, uncovered an abalone that was still beating!  Gross!  Awesome!

We ended the day in Roppongi, Tokyo’s nightlife district.  It was full of bars and high end restaurants.  We wanted to see what the kids do at night here and the answer is eat, drink and sometimes sing karaoke.  Seems about right.  There was an excellent store there called Don Quixote where they sold assorted craziness.

The best thing on sale was Joke Pocky.  It looks just like a regular box of Pocky, but when you offer it to someone and they try to pull out a stick of Pocky … they get a face full of Pocky!  Oh, snap!  You been served.  By Pocky.

First day in Tokyo: Shibuya, Harajuku, and Shinjuku
[ Posted Thu, 02 Feb 2012 06:36:00 ]

Hello, Tokyo!  You wacky son-of-a-shogun.  Today we started out our day in Shibuya, home of the famous eight-way (it could be less) all-way pedestrian crosswalk that is often seen in film, and a ramen place that Matt read about.

Since there are no street names in Tokyo, it took us a while, and several wrong turns, to find this nondescript ramen bar.  You enter and place your order with a vending machine. There are only three choices: regular, with egg, and with extra pork.  You buy your ticket, squeeze your way up to the 12-seat counter, and hand it to the friendly Japanese lady who will eventually give you your bowl of delicious ramen.

Ramen bar protocol seems to be this: 1) don’t dawdle; people are waiting for your seat, 2) don’t talk to each other, and 3) slurp as loud as you possibly can.  When all twelve of us at the counter were slurping strong, it sounded like “A Little Night Music” but wetter.

We then went to Tokyu Hands store in Shibuya based on Dalila’s recommendation.  It’s a really cool place — seven stories of leather goods, model trains, humidifiers, candy, pens, bath accessories and much, much more.  Matt bought a shaving mirror and I bought bath salts for the deep soaking tub in our hotel room.

From Shibuya, we headed to Harajuku because I’d seen an interesting street from the train as it went by Harajuku Station.  When we got there, we felt exceptionally old.  Teenagers crowded the pedestrian shopping street wearing fashions that made me want to wrap a coat around them — though this is true for most of Tokyo’s women.  It’s 34 degrees!  Wear pants and a good hat!  Your knees look frozen!  On the other hand, the women here are more healthy looking.  Matt says that maybe the women in Hong Kong look like they’re missing a good cheeseburger because the women in Tokyo ate it.  You be the judge of that comment.

We finished off the day in the downtown skyscrapers of Shinjuku.  We picked up a dinner of supermarket goodies — so mostly Japanese rice crackers, mochi balls and seaweed.

The monastery is about 30 minutes drive from Barcelona and set on top of Monserrat, a bizarre looking mountain with serrated conglomerate rock that sticks straight up from flat ground.  We were trying to figure out how the mountain could have gotten there and our best guess is seismic event.  Surely there are some geologists out there who can help us?

A cable car takes visitors from the base of the mountain about 2/3 of the way up, where there is a museum, cafe, visitor center and oh, also a monastery.  There are fantastic views from the cable car.  

If you want to go to the top, you can hike or take the funicular.  Since we were low on time, we took that to the top and barely had time to eat our last Iberico sandwiches before we begrudgingly made our way down.   We could see hikes out to cliffs and look outs; as well as stairs cut right into the mountain.  All we can do is promise to return.

We started our day with a leisurely stroll through the Eixample, the “addition”.  It’s a grid system of streets that was constructed when Barcelona exploded past its traditional Roman walls.  It’s also where the Moderniste architectural movement took hold.  We took a walking tour of several notable examples of Moderniste architecture, finishing up at Gaudi’s most famous work, the Sagrada Familia.  We are in agreement that he was 1) clearly a genius and 2) clearly disturbed.

We walked through the Christmas market in Rembrandtplein and then continued past the Botanical Garden and towards the Tropenmuseum or Tropical Museum.  The museum’s aim was originally to display the way of life of people in the Dutch colonies and not in a sensitive way.  Nowadays, the Dutch have done an about face and are critical of the way that the Dutch East India Company and supporting military forces acted in Indonesia, Suriname, etc.  Displays are about the culture and art from those countries and the history of Dutch interaction.

Haarlem
[ Posted Sat, 17 Dec 2011 15:30:00 ]

Today, we visited the town of Haarlem and realized that Haarlem is closer to Amsterdam than Harlem is to what was New Amsterdam in New York.   The train ride took about 16 minutes.  I was expecting a mini-Amsterdam, but Haarlem has its own vibe.  The streets are on a smaller scale, the homes and businesses a little more cozy and there are more gardens and flowers.  There was a market with produce (like mushrooms!), cheese, bread, fish, meats and gifts.  We ran into another Christmas concert, but it was a spirited choir instead of a full on televised production like in Dam Square.  On the whole, the city was charming.

The highlights of our visit were mostly food related again, quiche, hot chocolate, mint tea, crusty breads, gourmet garlic sausage, but we also visited the de Adriaan windmill museum where we got a personal tour of the working windmill.  The de Adriaan was a working windmill from 1778-1932 when it burned to the ground.  55 volunteer “windmill enthusiasts” have rebuilt the windmill in 2002 and now the windmill serves as a museum about how windmills work. 

No rain for our trip to Haarlem, but rain back in Amsterdam.  We’ll try again tomorrow with Amsterdam.
